Chapter 10: The Park Remembers the Dead

Author: drivenemo
Published: 2026-04-26
Previous Chapter Table of Contents Next Chapter

The staff break room was behind the maintenance office, past a soda machine with no power and a door that opened for Jophery without asking Mateo anything.

Mateo went in because his hands would not stop shaking.

He told himself he was looking for batteries.

That was mostly true.

The break room had a microwave, six plastic chairs, a sink full of mugs, a board of safety notices, and a cork strip crowded with photographs. Men on boats. Women holding babies. A boy in a school uniform with two front teeth missing. A wedding picture folded at the corner. A Polaroid of Tomás Araya standing beside a service cart with both thumbs up, pretending the cart was a race car.

Mateo took the Polaroid down and put it in his shirt pocket.

Not for a reason he could name.

Jophery Brown had three photographs.

One showed him in a baseball cap, holding a fish too small for the pride on his face. One showed a woman and two girls at a kitchen table. The youngest girl had birthday cake on her chin. The third photograph was Jophery with three other workers under the raptor service awning. Someone had written on the white border:

FIVE MINUTES, BROWN SAID.

Mateo did not laugh.

He searched Jophery’s cubby below the corkboard. Batteries, a clean pair of socks, a pack of gum, two pay stubs, a folded complaint form.

The complaint form was dated six days before Jophery died.

It said reader D-17 had opened for the wrong person during a drill. It said the fallback table was older than the live roster. It said temporary and transfer personnel were not being tested under degrade conditions. It said, in Jophery’s careful print:

IF THIS FAILS DURING A CONTAINMENT EVENT, SOMEONE WILL BE LOCKED WHERE THEY SHOULD NOT BE.

Dale Haskins had signed the bottom.

ACKNOWLEDGED.

No action line.

No repair line.

Acknowledged was a word people used when they wanted a problem to die on paper.

Mateo put the complaint form with the manifest and the printed access log. Evidence was too strong a word. Evidence belonged to people who could stand in a room and be believed.

He had paper.

Paper got wet.

The emergency light over the door blinked twice and turned steady red.

The PA came alive.

All remaining personnel shelter in designated secure rooms. Do not attempt exterior transit.

The message repeated in Spanish. The Spanish was worse.

Mateo looked at the secure-room sign on the back of the door. It pointed toward SECURITY READY ROOM B.

Jophery had drawn a line through that room on the route sheet.

NO WINDOWS. ONE READER. BAD HINGE.

Mateo chose the opposite way.

In the hallway, the access panel beside the break room showed a list of recent entries. It had not shown a list before. Maybe the system was waking up piece by piece. Maybe the dead were easier to count in order.

BROWN, JOPHERY - EAST DOCK OFFICE
BROWN, JOPHERY - PUMP HOUSE
BROWN, JOPHERY - SHUTTLE 4
BROWN, JOPHERY - COM-3
BROWN, JOPHERY - STAFF BREAK ROOM

At the bottom, the cursor blinked.

Then it added one more line.

BROWN, JOPHERY - RAPTOR SERVICE ENTRY

Mateo was not at raptor service.
